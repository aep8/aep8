Research Interests

My interests lie at the intersection of politics and technology, with a focus on the influence of digital tools on individual and mass political behavior. This includes exploring new forms of behavior that emerge from the digital environment, as well as examining how previously studied causal relationships manifest differently when occurring through online platforms. Additionally, I try to explore novel approaches to generating data through the use of digital tools. In my projects, I employ a wide set of quantitative tools, with a focus on experimental and survey methods.

Research Projects in Progress

Campaign Negativity and Political Participation in the Social Media Era

(With Asahi Obata) Do negative political campaigns lead to lower political participation? We revisit this question in the current era of multiple social and media platforms. While several studies have explored whether negative campaigns on traditional media lead to demobilization, we argue that this relationship is conditional on the media voters see negative campaigning on. We expect a demobilization effect to be larger when negative campaigning is fielded on a platform perceived as positive versus one where subjects already expect to see negative content.

Evidence On Fact-Checking, Learning, and Fake News Literacy

Literature on misinformation has focused mainly on the effectiveness of fake news debunking attempts. Still, no research has directly tested whether continuous exposure to fact-checking over time improves fake news detection skills. I propose a research design to test this question, with the expectation that exposure to fact-checking improves detection skills even among subjects that reject debunking attempts.

What Does “Taste-Based” Even Mean? Disentangling Partisan Sorting in Airbnb Rentals

Research has suggested that perceptions of partisanship influence economic decisions in a taste-based way. However, different definitions of "taste-based" point to different possible mechanisms. This project proposes a conjoint experiment that explores two alternative mechanisms for taste-based partisan sorting by exploiting features of Airbnb rentals.
